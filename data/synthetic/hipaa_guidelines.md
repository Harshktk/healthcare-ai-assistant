# Patient Privacy and HIPAA-Style Guidelines

**Document type:** Compliance summary
**Audience:** Patients and staff
**Last updated:** 2026-01-15

## 1. Protected health information

Protected health information, often called PHI, refers to any information that
identifies a patient and relates to their past, present, or future physical
or mental health, the provision of healthcare to them, or payment for that
care. Examples include the patient's name combined with a diagnosis, medical
record numbers, dates of service, billing details, and images that could
identify the patient.

## 2. When the practice may share PHI

The practice may use or share PHI without separate written authorisation in
the following situations:

- For treatment, including consultations between clinicians involved in the
  patient's care
- For payment, including submitting claims to insurance and verifying
  eligibility
- For routine healthcare operations, such as quality improvement, training,
  and accreditation
- When required by law, such as reporting certain communicable diseases or
  responding to a valid court order

For most other uses, including marketing or sharing PHI with an external
party, the practice will request written authorisation from the patient.

## 3. Patient rights

Patients have the right to:

- Receive a copy of this notice at the start of care
- Request and inspect their medical records, generally within thirty days
- Request a correction to information they believe is inaccurate
- Request that communications be sent to a specific address or phone number
- Receive a list of certain disclosures the practice has made of their PHI
- File a complaint with the practice or the relevant regulator without fear
  of retaliation

## 4. Minimum necessary

When the practice uses or shares PHI for non-treatment purposes, only the
minimum amount of information needed for the task is used. For example, a
billing query will not include full clinical notes if only diagnosis codes
are required.

## 5. Breach response

If the practice discovers a breach of unsecured PHI, it will notify the
affected patients without unreasonable delay, and in any case within sixty
days of discovery, in line with applicable law. The notification will
describe what happened, what types of information were involved, and the
steps the patient can take to protect themselves.

## 6. Speaking with family members

The practice will only discuss a patient's PHI with a family member or friend
if the patient is present and agrees, if the patient has previously
designated that person, or if the clinician judges, based on professional
judgement, that the disclosure is in the patient's best interest, for example
when the patient is incapacitated. Patients may at any time update or revoke
the list of people with whom information may be shared.

## 7. Telehealth and PHI

Telehealth visits are conducted on encrypted platforms and are not recorded
by default. Clinicians are expected to take a telehealth call from a private
location and to use headphones where appropriate to reduce the risk that
others can overhear PHI.
